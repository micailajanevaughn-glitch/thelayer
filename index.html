About Micaila

I spent 20+ years in architectural lighting — not just selling it, but living inside its full lifecycle: how a spec gets written, how a project gets won, how a team gets trained, how a system breaks down. I know exactly where the money gets lost, and I know what it looks like when the right capability meets the wrong process.

Off the portfolio: I'm a hiker, avid reader, Brazilian Jiu Jitsu practitioner, yogi, and kayaker. These aren't just escapes — they're disciplines that demand presence, adaptation, and respect for what you don't yet understand. I mentor and empower young women in tech and business, because the future belongs to people who can see what's missing and have the courage to build it. That mentorship mindset is the same one I bring to every engagement.

Teloric exists because I saw a pattern: most companies don't have an AI problem or a systems problem. They have a translation problem — and a people problem. The tools exist. The infrastructure is live. But somewhere between the executive decision and the team on the ground, adoption stalls because no one translated the vision into action. My work is to sit at that intersection, build that bridge, and mentor the people who make it real.

What Teloric Does
AI Readiness & Strategy
Most organizations aren't starting from zero — they already have tools, pilots, maybe a few internal champions. The gap is knowing which investments are worth scaling, how they connect to the systems you're already running, and whether a governance framework exists to protect the organization as it scales. That foundation is non-negotiable. That's where we start.
Business Intelligence Activation
Your data is already talking. Your BI tools are already running. The problem is that no one built the bridge between what the system can see and what your team actually needs to make decisions. We close that gap — and surface what's been sitting there the whole time.
AI Integration & Implementation
The tool is rarely the problem. The missing link is almost always the same: the AI doesn't talk to the systems your team lives in. We map the workflow, find the break points, and build the connections that make the investment actually work — with data security and access governance designed in from the start, not bolted on after.
Workforce Transformation
You can deploy the best AI in the world and watch it collect dust. The limiting factor is never the technology — it's the people who have to trust it, learn it, and eventually rely on it. We redesign how teams work from the inside out, around how they actually operate.
AI Literacy & Prompt Engineering
Most AI training teaches people what the tool can do. We teach people how to think in it. That difference shows up immediately — in adoption rates, output quality, and whether your investment survives contact with the real world. Built for the humans who have to use it every day, not just the ones who bought it.
Change Management
Buying the software is the easy part. Getting a team to actually change how they work — that requires someone who understands both what the technology demands and what the humans need. That's the work most implementations skip. And it's exactly where the ROI is lost or won.

You asked for my resume. I appreciated the patience — I wanted to think through what you're actually navigating before putting something in front of you. I was also at SXSW last week in the AI and emerging tech sessions, watching how organizations are restructuring around these systems in practice. That context felt relevant before writing.

Here's my read.

USESI operates across 14 companies — electrical distribution at the core, with lighting layered across most of the portfolio. SSG is implementing Eclipse, piloting enterprise LLMs, building BI infrastructure. The investment is real. But as those systems reach the lighting operations, they'll need someone who understands what lighting actually requires before any of it can be trained to serve it.

Lighting doesn't behave like the rest of the business. The spec process is different. Margin moves differently. Projects are relationship-driven and timing-sensitive. Most of the real knowledge lives in conversations and individual experience — not in structured systems. Until that reality is translated, the investment accelerates in the wrong direction.

From the outside, it also looks like some of the day-to-day visibility across the lighting operations still relies on manual handoffs and individual judgment. That's not unusual in strong, relationship-driven environments — but it does tend to concentrate load in a few key people and make it harder to step away or scale cleanly.

What's missing is someone who can operate in both worlds without translating down. Who understands lighting from the inside, and understands AI systems, organizational design, and enterprise infrastructure well enough to build something that actually fits — not a one-size solution that the whole portfolio has to work around.

My brother always said an ERP is only as strong as the number of people who touch it. No history. No pipeline. No patterns. Just an expensive shell that leadership can't see through and the field won't go near. Built intentionally around how lighting actually operates, that changes — across every company in the portfolio.

The solution isn't a new system. It's the layer that finally makes the existing investment work. When infrastructure is designed around how lighting actually operates — how projects move, how relationships drive decisions, how margin is protected — people use it naturally. Data enters as a byproduct of doing the work. History builds, pipeline becomes visible, patterns surface. Leadership sees across the portfolio for the first time. And that's the foundation you scale from.

What I'd see taking shape is a lighting-focused role within USESI, working closely with SSG but staying grounded in operations. Specifically:

A lighting-specific onboarding and training program — sitting alongside the existing leadership curriculum, combining scalable virtual modules with hands-on regional exposure, supported by AI-enabled knowledge systems that make it repeatable and searchable.

A system built around the activity that's already happening — so pipeline, margin, and client profitability surface automatically. Sales teams see their numbers in real time, commission potential becomes visible before the close, and the competitive instinct that drives good lighting people has something to run against.

When the data is structured intentionally, the organization stops being analog. Leadership sees the business clearly. Sales people see what's worth chasing. New talent sees a company that operates like it belongs in the next decade — not the last one.

The companies that survive the next twenty years in this industry won't be the ones that adopted the most tools. They'll be the ones that built the infrastructure to learn, adapt, and attract people who want to build something. That's what this makes possible.

The goal isn't more process. It's reduced friction, real visibility, and less load on the system — including you.

My background is about 20 years inside lighting across manufacturing, rep networks, and regional sales. The throughline has been understanding how things actually work and building the conditions for them to run better. More recently, I've been applying that same lens to AI and systems — starting with workflow and value flow first, then layering technology in where it genuinely helps.

I'm back now and would welcome a conversation this week or next to hear how this lands from your side.
